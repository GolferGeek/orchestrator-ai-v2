## Article I: Discovery Overview and Planning

### Section 1.1: Discovery Framework

#### 1.1.1 Purpose of Discovery

Discovery serves the following essential purposes in civil litigation:

1. **Fact Gathering**: Obtain information relevant to claims and defenses
2. **Witness Identification**: Identify persons with knowledge of relevant facts
3. **Document Collection**: Secure documentary evidence
4. **Expert Development**: Understand expert opinions and bases
5. **Case Evaluation**: Assess strengths and weaknesses
6. **Settlement Facilitation**: Enable informed settlement negotiations
7. **Trial Preparation**: Prepare for examination of witnesses and presentation of evidence

#### 1.1.2 Types of Written Discovery

| Discovery Type | Federal Rule | Purpose | Typical Limits |
|----------------|--------------|---------|----------------|
| Interrogatories | Rule 33 | Written questions requiring written answers | 25 (including subparts) |
| Requests for Production | Rule 34 | Request documents and tangible things | No numerical limit |
| Requests for Admission | Rule 36 | Establish undisputed facts | No numerical limit |
| Initial Disclosures | Rule 26(a)(1) | Mandatory disclosure of basic information | Required |
| Expert Disclosures | Rule 26(a)(2) | Disclosure of expert opinions | Required |
| Pretrial Disclosures | Rule 26(a)(3) | Disclosure of trial witnesses and exhibits | Required |

#### 1.1.3 Discovery Proportionality

Under FRCP Rule 26(b)(1), discovery must be:

1. **Relevant**: Reasonably calculated to lead to admissible evidence
2. **Proportional**: Considering:
   - Importance of issues at stake
   - Amount in controversy
   - Parties' relative resources
   - Importance of discovery in resolving issues
   - Burden/expense vs. likely benefit

### Section 1.2: Discovery Planning

#### 1.2.1 Initial Case Assessment Checklist

Before initiating discovery, evaluate:

- [ ] What facts must be proven to establish each element of claims/defenses?
- [ ] What documents are likely to exist and where?
- [ ] Who are the likely fact witnesses?
- [ ] What expert testimony will be needed?
- [ ] What discovery has already been provided (initial disclosures)?
- [ ] What is the discovery deadline?
- [ ] Are there any discovery limitations in the scheduling order?
- [ ] Are there privilege or confidentiality issues to consider?
- [ ] Is there ongoing document preservation duty?
- [ ] What is the anticipated discovery budget?

#### 1.2.2 Discovery Timeline Planning

| Phase | Federal Timeline | State Timeline | Action Items |
|-------|------------------|----------------|--------------|
| Initial Disclosures | 14 days after Rule 26(f) conference | Per local rule | Prepare client disclosures |
| Rule 26(f) Conference | 21 days before scheduling conference | Per local rule | Discuss discovery plan |
| Discovery Propounded | Any time after Rule 26(f) | Per local rule | Serve written discovery |
| Response Due | 30 days from service | 30-45 days (varies) | Calendar deadline |
| Expert Disclosures | Per scheduling order | Per scheduling order | Retain and disclose experts |
| Discovery Cutoff | Per scheduling order | Per scheduling order | Complete all discovery |

#### 1.2.3 Meet and Confer Requirements

Before filing discovery motions, parties must:

1. **Confer in Good Faith**: Actually discuss the dispute, not just exchange letters
2. **Identify Issues**: Specify what discovery is at issue
3. **Explain Position**: State basis for position
4. **Attempt Resolution**: Make reasonable effort to resolve without court
5. **Document Efforts**: Maintain record of meet-and-confer communications
6. **Certificate Required**: Many courts require certification of meet-and-confer

## Article II: Initial and Required Disclosures

### Section 2.1: Rule 26(a)(1) Initial Disclosures

#### 2.1.1 Timing and Content

Initial disclosures must be provided within 14 days after the Rule 26(f) conference, unless otherwise agreed or ordered.

**Required Contents:**

1. **Witnesses with Knowledge (26(a)(1)(A)(i))**:
   - Name, address, telephone number (if known)
   - Subjects of information
   - Include both favorable and unfavorable witnesses

2. **Documents and Tangible Things (26(a)(1)(A)(ii))**:
   - Description by category and location
   - Documents supporting claims/defenses
   - Copy or make available for inspection

3. **Damages Computation (26(a)(1)(A)(iii))**:
   - Computation of each category of damages
   - Documents supporting computation
   - Include lost profits, consequential damages, etc.

4. **Insurance Agreements (26(a)(1)(A)(iv))**:
   - Any insurance agreement under which insurer may be liable
   - Include all potentially applicable policies

#### 2.1.2 Initial Disclosure Checklist

**Witness Identification:**
- [ ] Review all client documents for names mentioned
- [ ] Interview client about all persons with knowledge
- [ ] Include corporate representatives with relevant knowledge
- [ ] Identify adverse witnesses known to client
- [ ] Include contact information if known
- [ ] Specify subject matter of each witness's knowledge
- [ ] Consider Rule 30(b)(6) designees

**Document Compilation:**
- [ ] Gather all relevant documents from client
- [ ] Organize by category/subject matter
- [ ] Create document index with descriptions
- [ ] Identify location of original documents
- [ ] Determine production format (native, PDF, paper)
- [ ] Review for privilege before disclosure
- [ ] Prepare privilege log if withholding documents

**Damages Calculation:**
- [ ] Identify each category of claimed damages
- [ ] Prepare computation methodology
- [ ] Gather supporting documentation
- [ ] Include expert reports if available
- [ ] Document all assumptions used
- [ ] Consider mitigation issues

**Insurance Review:**
- [ ] Request all potentially applicable policies
- [ ] Review policy declarations pages
- [ ] Identify all coverage towers
- [ ] Note any reservation of rights
- [ ] Include excess and umbrella policies

### Section 2.2: Expert Disclosures (Rule 26(a)(2))

#### 2.2.1 Expert Witness Categories

| Category | Disclosure Requirement | Report Required |
|----------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Retained Expert | Full disclosure with report | Yes, written report |
| Non-Retained Expert | Summary disclosure | No report required |
| Hybrid Expert (treating physician) | Depends on testimony scope | Case-by-case |

#### 2.2.2 Expert Report Requirements (Rule 26(a)(2)(B))

Required report contents:

1. **Complete Statement of Opinions**: All opinions to be expressed
2. **Basis and Reasons**: Basis and reasons for each opinion
3. **Supporting Data/Information**: All data/information considered
4. **Exhibits**: Exhibits to be used as summary or support
5. **Qualifications**: Witness qualifications including publications (10 years)
6. **Prior Testimony**: Cases testified in at trial/deposition (4 years)
7. **Compensation**: Statement of compensation for study/testimony

#### 2.2.3 Expert Disclosure Timing

Per scheduling order, typically:
- **Plaintiff's experts**: 90 days before discovery cutoff
- **Defendant's experts**: 60 days after plaintiff's disclosure
- **Rebuttal experts**: 30 days after defendant's disclosure

## Article III: Interrogatories

### Section 3.1: Interrogatory Fundamentals

#### 3.1.1 Rule 33 Requirements

- Limited to 25 interrogatories (including subparts) absent court order or stipulation
- Subparts count as separate interrogatories if not logically related
- Must be answered under oath within 30 days
- Answers must be signed by responding party
- Objections must be signed by attorney

#### 3.1.2 Types of Interrogatories

**Identification Interrogatories:**
- Witness identification
- Document identification
- Expert identification
- Corporate entity identification

**Substantive Interrogatories:**
- Factual basis for claims/defenses
- Contentions and legal theories
- Damages calculations
- Chronology of events

**Foundation Interrogatories:**
- Authentication foundation
- Business records foundation
- Expert foundation

### Section 3.2: Drafting Interrogatories Checklist

#### 3.2.1 Preliminary Considerations

- [ ] Review case file and pleadings thoroughly
- [ ] Identify elements requiring proof
- [ ] List information gaps to be filled
- [ ] Check local rules for format requirements
- [ ] Verify interrogatory limit (25 federal standard)
- [ ] Consider strategic timing
- [ ] Coordinate with document requests

#### 3.2.2 Standard Interrogatory Topics

**Party Identification:**
- [ ] Full legal name and aliases
- [ ] Business address and contact information
- [ ] Corporate status and state of incorporation
- [ ] Relationship to other parties
- [ ] Agents and representatives

**Witness Information:**
- [ ] Names of all persons with knowledge
- [ ] Contact information for each witness
- [ ] Subject matter of each witness's knowledge
- [ ] Relationship to parties
- [ ] Documents in possession of each witness

**Document Information:**
- [ ] Description of all relevant documents
- [ ] Location and custodian of documents
- [ ] Documents no longer in possession (why/when/where)
- [ ] Electronic storage information
- [ ] Document retention policies

**Factual Basis:**
- [ ] Facts supporting each claim/defense
- [ ] Chronology of relevant events
- [ ] Communications between parties
- [ ] Actions taken and by whom
- [ ] Basis for any denials in pleadings

**Damages:**
- [ ] Each category of damages claimed
- [ ] Method of calculating damages
- [ ] Supporting documentation
- [ ] Mitigation efforts
- [ ] Expert involvement in calculation

**Insurance:**
- [ ] All applicable insurance policies
- [ ] Policy limits and deductibles
- [ ] Coverage determinations
- [ ] Reservation of rights
- [ ] Other insureds

#### 3.2.3 Drafting Tips

**DO:**
- Use clear, precise language
- Define key terms
- Request identification of documents to be used in response
- Include "continuing duty to supplement" reminder
- Number consecutively
- Proofread carefully

**DON'T:**
- Use compound questions disguised as single interrogatories
- Ask legal conclusions instead of facts
- Request information already in disclosures
- Ask overly broad questions inviting objections
- Exceed the numerical limit

### Section 3.3: Responding to Interrogatories Checklist

#### 3.3.1 Upon Receipt

- [ ] Calendar response deadline (30 days from service)
- [ ] Send copy to client immediately
- [ ] Review for objectionable interrogatories
- [ ] Identify information needed from client
- [ ] Assess scope and burden of response
- [ ] Begin document search if needed

#### 3.3.2 Client Interview and Research

- [ ] Schedule client meeting promptly
- [ ] Prepare written summary for client review
- [ ] Gather relevant documents
- [ ] Interview relevant personnel
- [ ] Research corporate records
- [ ] Obtain expert input if needed

#### 3.3.3 Drafting Responses

**Format Requirements:**
- [ ] Restate each interrogatory before response
- [ ] Number responses to match interrogatories
- [ ] Include verification signature block
- [ ] Include attorney signature for objections

**Response Options:**
- [ ] Full answer
- [ ] Answer with objection (state objection, then answer)
- [ ] Objection only (sparingly, with specificity)
- [ ] Reference to documents (Rule 33(d) option)

**Common Objections:**
- Vague, ambiguous, or unintelligible
- Overly broad, unduly burdensome
- Compound question
- Calls for legal conclusion
- Privileged information
- Not reasonably calculated to lead to admissible evidence
- Exceeds numerical limit

#### 3.3.4 Response Quality Checklist

- [ ] Each answer is complete and accurate
- [ ] All responsive information is included
- [ ] No inadvertent privilege waiver
- [ ] Objections are specific and supportable
- [ ] Information is presented clearly
- [ ] Client has reviewed and verified
- [ ] Proper signatures obtained
- [ ] Response is timely served

## Article IV: Requests for Production

### Section 4.1: Document Request Fundamentals

#### 4.1.1 Rule 34 Requirements

- No numerical limit (but proportionality applies)
- Response due within 30 days of service
- Must produce documents as kept in ordinary course OR organized by request category
- Must permit inspection of tangible things
- Electronic documents must be produced in native or reasonably usable form

#### 4.1.2 Scope of Production

Documents subject to production include:
- Paper documents
- Electronic documents
- Photographs and images
- Audio and video recordings
- Databases and structured data
- Metadata
- Text messages and instant messages
- Social media content
- Voicemail

### Section 4.2: Drafting Document Requests Checklist

#### 4.2.1 Preliminary Planning

- [ ] Review pleadings for relevant categories
- [ ] Identify key custodians
- [ ] Consider date ranges
- [ ] Plan document management system
- [ ] Coordinate with interrogatories
- [ ] Consider ESI format specifications
- [ ] Budget for document review

#### 4.2.2 Standard Request Categories

**Core Documents:**
- [ ] All documents referenced in pleadings
- [ ] All documents supporting claims/defenses
- [ ] All documents identified in initial disclosures
- [ ] All documents provided to experts
- [ ] All correspondence between parties

**Communications:**
- [ ] Emails between specified parties/custodians
- [ ] Internal communications regarding subject matter
- [ ] Communications with third parties
- [ ] Meeting notes and agendas
- [ ] Text messages and instant messages

**Business Records:**
- [ ] Contracts and agreements
- [ ] Invoices and payment records
- [ ] Personnel files (where relevant)
- [ ] Policies and procedures
- [ ] Corporate minutes and resolutions
- [ ] Financial statements

**Technical Documents:**
- [ ] Specifications and designs
- [ ] Testing records
- [ ] Quality control documents
- [ ] Maintenance records
- [ ] Incident reports

**Insurance and Financial:**
- [ ] Insurance policies and correspondence
- [ ] Claim files
- [ ] Financial projections
- [ ] Loss calculations

### Section 4.3: ESI-Specific Considerations

#### 4.3.1 ESI Protocol Checklist

- [ ] Identify sources of ESI early
- [ ] Negotiate form of production (native vs. image)
- [ ] Specify metadata requirements
- [ ] Address deduplication approach
- [ ] Establish search term protocol
- [ ] Define custodian scope
- [ ] Address cost allocation

#### 4.3.2 Form of Production Options

| Format | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|--------|------------|---------------|
| Native | Preserves metadata, functionality | May be manipulable |
| PDF/TIFF | Easy to review, Bates stamp | Loses functionality |
| Paper | Simple, traditional | Loses metadata, ESI features |
| Database/Load File | Efficient review | Requires review platform |

### Section 4.4: Responding to Production Requests Checklist

#### 4.4.1 Document Collection

- [ ] Issue litigation hold if not already done
- [ ] Identify all potentially responsive custodians
- [ ] Map data sources (email, network, local, cloud)
- [ ] Collect from all sources
- [ ] Preserve chain of custody
- [ ] Document collection methodology

#### 4.4.2 Document Review Process

**First-Pass Review:**
- [ ] Remove clearly non-responsive documents
- [ ] Flag privileged documents
- [ ] Identify potentially responsive documents
- [ ] Classify by relevance level

**Second-Pass Review:**
- [ ] Confirm responsiveness determination
- [ ] Apply privilege designations
- [ ] Redact sensitive information
- [ ] Prepare privilege log entries
- [ ] Quality check sample

**Final Review:**
- [ ] Partner/supervisor review of key documents
- [ ] Final privilege determination
- [ ] Approve production set
- [ ] Generate privilege log

#### 4.4.3 Production Mechanics

- [ ] Prepare response letter
- [ ] Generate Bates numbers
- [ ] Apply confidentiality designations
- [ ] Prepare production media
- [ ] Create production index/log
- [ ] Serve on all parties
- [ ] Retain copy of production

## Article V: Requests for Admission

### Section 5.1: RFA Fundamentals

#### 5.1.1 Rule 36 Requirements

- No numerical limit (but proportionality applies)
- Response due within 30 days
- Matters admitted are conclusively established
- Must admit, deny, or explain inability to admit/deny
- Failure to respond = deemed admitted

#### 5.1.2 Strategic Use of RFAs

RFAs are particularly effective for:
- Establishing undisputed facts
- Authenticating documents
- Establishing genuineness of signatures
- Narrowing issues for trial
- Laying foundation for summary judgment
- Pinning down opponent's positions

### Section 5.2: Drafting RFAs Checklist

#### 5.2.1 Types of Requests

**Factual Admissions:**
- [ ] Undisputed background facts
- [ ] Dates, times, locations
- [ ] Identity of parties/witnesses
- [ ] Existence of relationships

**Document Authentication:**
- [ ] Document is genuine
- [ ] Signature is authentic
- [ ] Document was sent/received
- [ ] Document is business record

**Legal Conclusions/Applications:**
- [ ] Party is owner of property
- [ ] Contract was breached
- [ ] Duty of care existed
- [ ] Damages were caused by X

#### 5.2.2 Drafting Tips

**DO:**
- Make each request simple and unambiguous
- State facts, not arguments
- Include specific documents by attachment
- Use RFAs to establish facts for MSJ
- Time strategically (late enough to know facts, early enough to use)

**DON'T:**
- Compound multiple facts into one request
- Use RFAs as disguised interrogatories
- Ask matters you know will be denied
- Forget to attach referenced documents

### Section 5.3: Responding to RFAs Checklist

#### 5.3.1 Response Options

| Response | When Appropriate | Effect |
|----------|------------------|--------|
| Admit | Fact is true | Conclusively established |
| Deny | Fact is false | Must be proven at trial |
| Qualified Admission | Part true, part false | Admit true parts, deny false |
| Lack of Information | Cannot determine truth | Must state efforts to investigate |
| Objection | Request improper | Must be specific |

#### 5.3.2 Response Process

- [ ] Calendar response deadline immediately
- [ ] Review each request carefully
- [ ] Research facts before responding
- [ ] Investigate any claimed lack of information
- [ ] Consult with client on disputed facts
- [ ] Draft responses with specificity
- [ ] Review for inadvertent admissions
- [ ] Obtain client approval
- [ ] Sign and serve timely

#### 5.3.3 Important Warnings

- **Failure to respond = ADMISSION**: Take deadlines seriously
- **Partial admission/denial**: Must specify what is admitted/denied
- **Lack of information response**: Must describe investigation undertaken
- **Objection only**: Risky; may be deemed waiver
- **Amendment**: Can move to amend admission, but courts are strict

## Article VI: Discovery Disputes and Enforcement

### Section 6.1: Meet and Confer Procedures

#### 6.1.1 Pre-Motion Requirements

Before filing any discovery motion:

1. **Contact Opposing Counsel**: Phone call preferred; document attempt
2. **Identify Specific Issues**: Be precise about what is disputed
3. **Exchange Positions**: Share legal basis for positions
4. **Propose Solutions**: Offer compromise where appropriate
5. **Document Exchange**: Follow up in writing
6. **Allow Time**: Give reasonable time to respond
7. **Certify Efforts**: Include in motion if filing required

#### 6.1.2 Meet and Confer Letter Template

```
Re: [Case Name] - Discovery Dispute

Dear Counsel:

This letter memorializes our meet-and-confer efforts regarding [specific discovery at issue].

On [date], we served [specific discovery]. Your response, received on [date], [describe deficiency - e.g., objected to interrogatory No. X, produced no documents in response to Request No. Y].

Our position is that [state basis for position with legal support].

We propose the following resolution: [specific proposal].

Please respond by [date] so we may avoid the need for court intervention.

[Signature]
```

### Section 6.2: Discovery Motions

#### 6.2.1 Motion to Compel Checklist

**Preparation:**
- [ ] Verify meet-and-confer certification requirements
- [ ] Compile meet-and-confer correspondence
- [ ] Identify specific discovery at issue
- [ ] Research applicable rules and case law
- [ ] Calculate deadline for motion

**Motion Contents:**
- [ ] Identify discovery requests at issue
- [ ] State objections/deficiencies in responses
- [ ] Explain why responses are inadequate
- [ ] Attach discovery requests and responses
- [ ] Attach meet-and-confer correspondence
- [ ] Request specific relief
- [ ] Request fees and costs

**Supporting Brief:**
- [ ] Procedural history
- [ ] Relevance of requested discovery
- [ ] Inadequacy of responses
- [ ] Applicable law
- [ ] Response to anticipated objections
- [ ] Proportionality analysis

#### 6.2.2 Motion for Protective Order Checklist

**Grounds for Protection:**
- [ ] Annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression
- [ ] Undue burden or expense
- [ ] Trade secret protection
- [ ] Confidential business information
- [ ] Privacy concerns
- [ ] Privilege protection

**Motion Contents:**
- [ ] Specific discovery at issue
- [ ] Specific protection requested
- [ ] Good cause statement
- [ ] Supporting declaration/affidavit
- [ ] Proposed protective order language

### Section 6.3: Discovery Sanctions

#### 6.3.1 Types of Sanctions (Rule 37)

| Violation | Potential Sanctions |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Failure to make disclosures | Exclusion of witness/evidence |
| Failure to respond | Motion to compel + fees; later: contempt |
| Incomplete response | Motion to compel + fees |
| Failure to obey order | Issue/fact established; strike claims/defenses; dismissal/default |
| Spoliation | Adverse inference; exclusion; dismissal |

#### 6.3.2 Protecting Against Sanctions

- [ ] Calendar all deadlines
- [ ] Respond to all discovery timely
- [ ] Object specifically (not boilerplate)
- [ ] Supplement responses as required
- [ ] Preserve documents (litigation hold)
- [ ] Document preservation efforts
- [ ] Communicate with client regularly
- [ ] Meet and confer in good faith

## Article VII: Privilege and Work Product

### Section 7.1: Privilege Review

#### 7.1.1 Attorney-Client Privilege Elements

To assert privilege, communication must:
1. Be between client and attorney (or their agents)
2. Be made for purpose of obtaining legal advice
3. Be confidential (not shared with third parties)
4. Not be waived

#### 7.1.2 Work Product Protection Elements

Work product protection applies to:
1. Documents and tangible things
2. Prepared in anticipation of litigation
3. By or for a party or representative

**Types:**
- Ordinary work product: Can be overcome by substantial need + undue hardship
- Opinion work product: Generally absolute protection (mental impressions)

### Section 7.2: Privilege Log Requirements

#### 7.2.1 Required Information

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Date | Date of document |
| Author | Person who created document |
| Recipients | All persons who received document |
| Description | General subject (without revealing privileged content) |
| Privilege | Specific privilege asserted |
| Basis | Basis for privilege claim |

#### 7.2.2 Privilege Log Best Practices

- [ ] Log all withheld documents (even partial redactions)
- [ ] Provide sufficient detail for challenge assessment
- [ ] Group related documents logically
- [ ] Update log as additional documents identified
- [ ] Consider categorical logging for large volumes
- [ ] Review log for inadvertent disclosures

### Section 7.3: Clawback Agreements

#### 7.3.1 Federal Rule of Evidence 502

FRE 502(d) order provisions:
- Inadvertent production does not waive privilege
- Applies to all parties and non-parties
- Binding on other courts

**Benefits:**
- Reduces cost of privilege review
- Provides protection for inadvertent disclosure
- Encourages reasonable (not perfect) review

#### 7.3.2 Sample Clawback Language

```
Pursuant to Federal Rule of Evidence 502(d), the inadvertent production
of any document or information protected by attorney-client privilege
or work product doctrine shall not constitute a waiver of the privilege
or protection as to the producing party in this or any other federal or
state proceeding. Upon notification of inadvertent production, the
receiving party shall promptly return or destroy all copies of the
identified materials and may not use such materials for any purpose.
```

## Appendix B: Common Discovery Objections Reference

| Objection | Basis | Response Strategy |
|-----------|-------|-------------------|
| Relevance | Not reasonably calculated to lead to admissible evidence | Show logical connection to claims/defenses |
| Overbroad | Requests information beyond scope of case | Narrow scope; negotiate limitations |
| Unduly burdensome | Burden outweighs likely benefit | Demonstrate proportionality; offer alternatives |
| Vague/Ambiguous | Request unclear | Clarify terms; offer definitions |
| Compound | Multiple requests in one | Separate components; prioritize |
| Privileged | Attorney-client or work product | Challenge basis; request privilege log |
| Trade secret | Confidential business information | Offer protective order; limit access |
